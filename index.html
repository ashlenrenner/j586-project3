Introduction

As the U.S. recovered from the initial shock of the Great Recession, many schools across the country struggled to provide their students with the resources they needed to succeed. The U.S. was falling behind in education as reading, writing and math scores steadily decreased, and schools had to cut back their budgets. States were awarded nearly $3.5 billion in School Improvement Grant (SIG) funds in 2010 to turn around their persistently lowest-achieving schools. Since then, studies have shown SIG schools have improved, but even as the country eased out of the Recession, schools continue to apply for aid.

Improvement Models

Each SIG school is required to choose one of the four improvement methods set by the U.S. Department of Education.

Transformation Model

The Transformation Model requires schools to "replace the principal, develop a teacher-and leader-evaluation system that takes student progress into account, introduce significant instructional reforms, increase learning time, and provide flexibility and support."
Source: U.S. Department of Education

Turnaround Model

The Turnaround Model requires schools to "replace the principal and no less than 50 percent of the staff, introduce significant instructional reforms, increase learning time, and provide flexinility and support."
Source: U.S. Department of Education

Restart Model

The Restart Model details schools reopen "under the management of a charter school operator, a charter management organization or an education management organiztion."
Source: U.S. Department of Education

School Closure

SIG Schools that follow the School Closure Model close entirely and "reassign students to a higher-achieving schools."
Source: U.S. Department of Education

Performance in Math in Primary Schools

According to a study by the Council of the Great City Schools, math test scores improved after inner-city schools received School Improvement Grants. The study analyzed more than 100 schools that received grants during the 2009-10 academic year, schools eligible for grants but did not receive them, and schools not eligible for grants.
Source: U.S. Department of Education, Council of the Great City Schools
